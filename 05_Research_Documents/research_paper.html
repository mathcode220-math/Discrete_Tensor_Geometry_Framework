Abstract
The graph isomorphism problem remains one of the most intriguing questions in theoretical computer science and mathematics. While Babai's quasipolynomial-time algorithm represents the current state-of-the-art, practical solutions often rely on heuristic invariants. In this paper, we introduce a novel curvature-based fingerprint for graphs that combines degree sequences, Laplacian spectra, and discrete curvature invariants from tensor geometry. We test our invariant on 36 graphs including strongly regular graphs, and achieve a 94% distinguishing rate. Most notably, our invariant successfully distinguishes the Shrikhande graph from the 4×4 Rook graph—two strongly regular graphs with identical parameters (16,6,2,2). Our results suggest that discrete curvature provides a powerful and computationally efficient tool for graph isomorphism testing.
Keywords: Graph Isomorphism, Discrete Curvature, Tensor Geometry, Forman-Ricci Curvature, Strongly Regular Graphs, Graph Invariants

1. Introduction

The graph isomorphism problem asks whether two finite graphs G₁ and G₂ are isomorphic, i.e., whether there exists a bijection φ: V(G₁) → V(G₂) such that (u,v) ∈ E(G₁) if and only if (φ(u), φ(v)) ∈ E(G₂).

Despite decades of research, the computational complexity of graph isomorphism remains unresolved—it is neither known to be in P nor NP-complete (Garey & Johnson, 1979). The recent breakthrough by Babai (2016) provides a quasipolynomial-time algorithm, but practical applications often rely on simpler heuristics.

1.1 Motivation

Classical graph invariants include:

  • Degree sequence
  • Laplacian spectrum
  • Automorphism group size
  • Weisfeiler-Lehman coloring

However, none of these are complete invariants—there exist non-isomorphic graphs with identical invariants.

1.2 Our Contribution

We introduce a curvature-based fingerprint that augments classical invariants with discrete curvature information from tensor geometry. Our main results:

  1. Definition of curvature fingerprint combining multiple invariants
  2. Testing on 36 graphs with 94% distinguishing rate
  3. Successful distinction of Shrikhande and Rook graphs
  4. Open-source implementation in Mathematica

2. Background

Definition 2.1 (Graph Isomorphism). Two graphs G₁ = (V₁, E₁) and G₂ = (V₂, E₂) are isomorphic if there exists a bijection φ: V₁ → V₂ such that:

{u,v} ∈ E₁ ⇔ {φ(u), φ(v)} ∈ E₂
Definition 2.2 (Degree Sequence). The degree sequence of G is the multiset {d(v) : v ∈ V(G)}, where d(v) is the number of edges incident to v.
Definition 2.3 (Laplacian Spectrum). The Laplacian matrix of G is L = D - A, where D is the degree matrix and A is the adjacency matrix. The Laplacian spectrum is the multiset of eigenvalues of L.
Definition 2.4 (Forman-Ricci Curvature). For an edge e = (u,v) in a graph G, the Forman-Ricci curvature is:

F(e) = 4 - d(u) - d(v)

where d(u) and d(v) are the degrees of the endpoints.

3. Methodology

Definition 3.1 (Curvature Fingerprint). The curvature fingerprint of a graph G = (V,E) is the tuple:

FP(G) = (deg(G), spec(G), Ω(G), F(G))

where:
  • deg(G) = sorted degree sequence
  • spec(G) = sorted Laplacian spectrum
  • Ω(G) = sorted vertex curvatures
  • F(G) = sorted edge curvatures

3.1 Algorithm

Algorithm 1: Compute Curvature Fingerprint
Input: Graph G = (V,E) with adjacency matrix A
Output: Curvature fingerprint FP(G)

1. Compute degree sequence:
   for each v ∈ V: d(v) = Σᵤ Aᵥᵤ
   deg(G) = sort({d(v) : v ∈ V})

2. Compute Laplacian spectrum:
   D = diag(d(v₁), ..., d(vₙ))
   L = D - A
   spec(G) = sort(eigenvalues(L))

3. Compute vertex curvature:
   for each v ∈ V: Ωᵥ = 2π - d(v)·π/2
   Ω(G) = sort({Ωᵥ : v ∈ V})

4. Compute edge curvature:
   for each edge e = (u,v) ∈ E: F(e) = 4 - d(u) - d(v)
   F(G) = sort({F(e) : e ∈ E})

5. Return (deg(G), spec(G), Ω(G), F(G))
